The current pipeline: alignJob finds the phoneme covering each frame midpoint, and for non-SIL/noise phonemes with F1/F2 present writes getLunaBrightness(label, f1, f2) — a z-score of F1/F2 against Luna McNulty's per-phoneme corpus stats, combined as (0.73·z1 + 0.27·z2)/3 + 0.5. TranscriptStore then reports the chunk-level mean over all frames that got a value. Here's what I'd change, ordered by expected impact.

## Reliability against imperfect alignment

1. Use only sonorant frames. The reference table includes stops and fricatives, but formant tracks during obstruents aren't vocal-tract resonances in any useful sense, and Luna's own stats say so — F1 stdev for /f/ is 469 Hz vs ~100–170 for vowels, so those z-scores are mostly noise around 0.5 (dilution) or garbage (spikes). The pitch gate already excludes most unvoiced frames, but voiced obstruents (/b d g v z ð/) and pitch bleeding across boundaries still contribute. Restrict to vowels, approximants, nasals, and laterals — you already have the class available either via pg16 on the aligned phoneme or an ARPABET vowel set. This also shrinks the damage a misaligned label can do: confusing one vowel for a neighboring vowel moves the z-score modestly; treating a /t/ burst as a vowel does not.
2. Drop boundary frames. Alignment endpoints are the least trustworthy part of the output — extendSoftBoundaries deliberately widens each phoneme into adjacent frames, so label bleed at edges is systematic, not occasional. Score only the interior of each phoneme's span (e.g. middle 50–60%, keeping at least the single midpoint frame for short phones). Most of the "alignment is often imperfect" pain is concentrated in exactly the frames this discards.
3. Aggregate robustly: per-phoneme median, then chunk-level median. The current flat frame mean has two problems: one octave-jumped or F2/F3-swapped formant frame drags the whole chunk, and long vowels dominate purely by frame count while contributing no independent evidence beyond a point. Take the median across a phoneme's interior frames, then aggregate per-chunk across phoneme instances (median, or a trimmed mean). This makes the score insensitive both to formant-tracker glitches and to alignment span errors that stretch a phoneme over its neighbor.
4. Use the confidence you already compute. PhonemeTimestamp.confidence comes out of calculateConfidences and is currently ignored by the brightness path. Gate (skip phonemes below a threshold) or weight per-phoneme contributions by it — a low-confidence phoneme is exactly one whose label or span is suspect. With the partial-align plan, the SW normalizedScore gives you a chunk-level trust signal too.
5. Minimum-evidence rule. After the gates, if fewer than N usable phoneme instances remain (say 3), show nothing rather than a percentage — a "62% bright" derived from one misaligned /ə/ is worse than no number. This fits the honest-hedging register of the rest of the UI.

## Matching perception better

6. Score in log-frequency (ratio) space, framed as formant spacing. What listeners track as "resonance"/vocal size is close to a multiplicative shift of the formant pattern (vocal-tract length), not additive Hz offsets. Compute per-formant log(f_measured / f_ref_median) and combine — this is effectively an apparent-VTL estimate. It's more perceptually linear (100 Hz at F1=300 is huge; at F2=2000 it's marginal — Hz-domain z-scores only partially capture that through stdevs), and it makes contributions from different vowels commensurable, so cross-phoneme aggregation gets cleaner too. Reliability and perception improvements compound here.
7. Reconsider F3 for sonorants. The comment notes F3 was zeroed on acousticgender.space after corpus analysis — plausibly a tracker-quality issue in that corpus rather than a perceptual finding. F3 is less phoneme-dependent than F1/F2 and is a strong speaker-size cue, which makes it valuable precisely when the label is uncertain: an F3-inclusive ratio estimate degrades gracefully under misalignment. Frames already carry f3. Worth an experiment before trusting the zero weight.
8. Calibrate the output mapping. (w1·z1 + w2·z2)/3 + 0.5 is a linear squash with an arbitrary /3; the displayed percentage has no stable meaning across the range. Map the combined statistic through a percentile/logistic fit against the reference distribution instead, so "70%" means something consistent and the ends don't saturate weirdly.
9. You have labeled validation data already. The Kokoro reference clips (per-sentence, per-voice, with femme/masc/enby voice labels) are a ready-made test set: run each variant of the score over them and check (a) voices order consistently with their labels, (b) within-voice variance across sentences is low, (c) the score is stable when you artificially jitter alignment boundaries by ±1–2 frames. That last test directly measures the "reliable under imperfect alignment" goal, and it's how I'd pick thresholds (confidence gate, trim fraction, N) rather than guessing.

If you want a minimal first package: sonorants-only + interior-frames + per-phoneme-then-chunk median + confidence gate (items 1–4) is all local to alignJob's loop and TranscriptStore.computeDerived, changes no data tables, and I'd expect it to remove most of the visible flakiness. The log-space/VTL reframing (items 6–8) is the deeper perceptual change and worth validating against the reference clips before shipping.
